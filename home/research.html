Core Principles

Three ideas this system is built on.

Not productivity hacks. Behavioral science applied to the specific context of student study behavior.

01
The problem is emotional, not motivational
Students don't procrastinate because they lack willpower. They procrastinate because tasks trigger negative emotions — anxiety, boredom, self-doubt. The brain seeks relief through distraction. This is task aversion, not laziness.
Sirois & Pychyl, 2013
02
Starting is the hardest part — so make it tiny
Implementation intentions (if-then plans) dramatically increase task completion. "When I sit down, I will study for 5 minutes" bypasses the emotional resistance that kills larger intentions. Small commitments that are kept build neural pathways.
Gollwitzer, 1999
03
Visible time creates honest urgency
When time is invisible, the brain systematically underestimates duration — enabling procrastination. Making time physically visible (sand falling) activates temporal awareness without the anxiety of a digital countdown. Urgency without threat.
Steel & König, 2006
Academic References

Seven studies. Seven design decisions.

Each reference maps directly to a feature in the system. Not decoration — documentation.

01
Implementation Intentions: Strong Effects of Simple Plans
Gollwitzer, P.M. (1999) — American Psychologist, 54(7), 493–503
If-then planning dramatically increases goal completion by pre-deciding responses to anticipated obstacles. Specific plans bypass the motivational failures that kill vague intentions.
→ Used in: Pause modal design · 5-minute rescue mode · "Name one thing" prompt
doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.54.7.493 ↗
02
Integrating Theories of Motivation (Temporal Motivation Theory)
Steel, P. & König, C.J. (2006) — Academy of Management Review, 31(4), 889–913
Motivation is a function of the expected value of a task and its temporal distance. Tasks far in the future feel less urgent regardless of importance. Visual time pressure closes this gap.
→ Used in: Sand timer design · time pressure psychology · urgency without anxiety
doi.org/10.5465/amr.2006.22527462 ↗
03
Procrastination and the Priority of Short-Term Mood Regulation
Sirois, F. & Pychyl, T. (2013) — Social & Personality Psychology Compass, 7(2), 115–127
Procrastination is primarily an emotion regulation problem, not a time management problem. Students avoid tasks because they trigger negative affect. Relief-seeking through distraction feels better short-term but compounds the problem.
→ Used in: Beginner distraction intervention · non-judgmental pause modal tone · reward framing
doi.org/10.1111/spc3.12011 ↗
04
Nasal Respiration Entrains Human Limbic Oscillations and Modulates Cognitive Function
Zelano, C. et al. (2016) — Journal of Neuroscience, 36(49), 12448–12467
Breathing rhythm synchronizes with neural oscillations in the limbic system. Slow, rhythmic breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system — reducing cortisol and creating conditions for sustained focus.
→ Used in: Timer circle breathing animation (4-second pulse cycle)
doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.2586-16.2016 ↗
05
Über das Behalten von erledigten und unerledigten Handlungen (Zeigarnik Effect)
Zeigarnik, B. (1927) — Psychologische Forschung, 9, 1–85
Incomplete tasks create persistent mental tension — a cognitive intrusion that occupies working memory until resolved. Intermediate learners studying many subjects simultaneously carry dozens of open loops, generating chronic background anxiety.
→ Used in: "Name one thing" intervention · understanding intermediate overwhelm · task decomposition design
06
Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience
Csikszentmihalyi, M. (1990) — Harper & Row, New York. ISBN: 978-0-06-092043-2
Flow states occur in the narrow band between boredom (challenge too low) and anxiety (challenge too high). The timer's design targets this band — creating enough time pressure to prevent boredom without the anxiety of a hard deadline.
→ Used in: Timer difficulty calibration · role-based session length design
07
Procrastination, Emotion Regulation, and Well-Being
Pychyl, T.A. & Sirois, F.M. (2016) — In Procrastination, Health, and Well-Being. Academic Press, 163–188
The distinction between difficulty-based procrastination (beginners) and pressure-based avoidance (intermediate learners) requires fundamentally different interventions. Treating both identically is why most productivity tools fail students.
→ Used in: Role system design · Beginner vs Intermediate behavioral distinction · pause modal branching
doi.org/10.1016/B978-0-12-802862-9.00008-6 ↗
